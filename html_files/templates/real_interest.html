For the first two calculations, we assumed no inflation. Inflation is the rate at which goods become more expensive over time. The government typically uses a consumer price index (CPI) to track inflation. To calculate CPI, the government takes a set basket of goods and monitors the change in historical pricing.

Inflation detracts from returns. For example, if a stock increases in value 10%, but inflation is 2%, the real value of the stock increases by roughly 8%. The real return is the stock's increase in value, which is different than its increase in price. The US Federal Reserve aims to keep inflation around 2% a year, though inflation has been much higher than 2% in recent times.
